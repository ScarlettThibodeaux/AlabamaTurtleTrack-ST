‚≠ê Alabama Red-bellied Turtle

Pseudemys alabamensis

Status: Endangered (Federal & State)

Habitat: Mobile-Tensaw River Delta, primarily in fresh and brackish water with high aquatic vegetation. They rely heavily on sandy river banks for nesting.

Life Expectancy: Estimated over 30 years.

Conservation Facts

  • State Reptile: This is Alabama's official State Reptile!
  • Primary Threat: Road Mortality. Females must cross busy roadways (like the Mobile Causeway) to reach nesting sites, leading to high adult fatality rates.
  • Nesting Sites: Destruction and degradation of the sandy nesting habitats are critical issues.

üåä Flattened Musk Turtle

Sternotherus depressus

Status: Threatened (Federal)

Habitat: Endemic (only found) in the upper Black Warrior River drainage system. Prefers clear, fast-flowing streams with abundant underwater rock crevices.

Life Expectancy: 20 to 40 years.

Conservation Facts

  • Unique Adaptation: Its namesake "flattened" shell is an evolutionary trait that allows it to easily hide in the narrow spaces between submerged boulders.
  • Primary Threat: Water Quality & Siltation. This turtle relies heavily on aquatic snails for food. Pollution and runoff destroy both the habitat and their food source.
  • Size: This is one of America's smallest turtles, rarely exceeding 4.3 inches in shell length.

üèñÔ∏è Loggerhead Sea Turtle

Caretta caretta

Status: Threatened (Northwest Atlantic DPS)

Habitat: Warm, temperate and tropical oceans. Females nest on the sandy beaches of Coastal Alabama.

Life Expectancy: 50 to 70+ years.

Conservation Facts

  • Massive Jaw: Named for its massive head and jaw muscles used to crush the shells of prey like clams and crabs.
  • Primary Threat: Bycatch (getting accidentally caught in fishing gear) and light pollution on nesting beaches that disorients hatchlings.
  • Unique Tracks: Leaves an alternating, asymmetrical track pattern in the sand when nesting.

üåä Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle

Lepidochelys kempii

Status: Endangered (Federal)

Habitat: Primarily the Gulf of Mexico. They are sometimes found in Alabama's coastal waters, though nesting rarely occurs here.

Size: Smallest marine turtle in the world, averaging only 2 feet long.

Conservation Facts

  • Arribadas: This species is famous for synchronized mass nesting events called *arribadas* (Spanish for "arrival").
  • Vulnerability: Due to their dependence on the Gulf of Mexico, they are highly vulnerable to oil spills and habitat degradation in the region.
  • Conservation: Use of Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs) in shrimping nets is critical for reducing accidental drowning.

üèúÔ∏è Gopher Tortoise (Land Dweller)

Gopherus polyphemus

Status: Threatened (in the Western portion of its range, including Alabama)

Habitat: Sandy, well-drained upland habitats, particularly longleaf pine forests in southern Alabama (a terrestrial terrain).

Keystone Species: Their extensive burrows provide essential shelter for over 350 other species from predators and fire.

Turtle vs. Tortoise Fact

  • Body Type: Unlike the aquatic turtles, the Gopher Tortoise has stumpy, club-like legs and a highly domed shell, perfectly adapted for digging deep burrows.
  • Burrow Depth: A single Gopher Tortoise burrow can be up to 48 feet long and 10 feet deep!
  • Biggest Threat: Habitat loss and fragmentation due to human development and agriculture.

üå≥ Turtle vs. Tortoise & Land vs. Sea üåä

Not all shelled reptiles are the same! Their body shape is perfectly adapted to their environment.

Turtle vs. Tortoise (General)

Feature TURTLE (Aquatic/Semi-aquatic) TORTOISE (Terrestrial, e.g., Gopher Tortoise)
Limbs Webbed feet or flippers for swimming. Stumpy, club-like legs for walking and heavy burrowing.
Shell Flatter, lighter, streamlined. Highly domed, heavy, protective.

Freshwater Turtle vs. Sea Turtle

Feature FRESHWATER TURTLE (e.g., Red-bellied) SEA TURTLE (e.g., Loggerhead)
Land Use Comes on land to nest, bask, and sometimes travel. Females only come ashore to nest; otherwise fully marine.
Diet Omnivorous or Herbivorous (aquatic plants). Carnivorous (crabs, jellyfish, mollusks).

üî¨ Cool Science & Fun Facts

  • Flattened for Survival: The Flattened Musk Turtle has an exceptionally flat shell‚Äîan adaptation allowing it to hide in the narrow crevices of rocky streambeds.
  • Temperature Control: The sex of sea turtle hatchlings is determined by nest temperature (TSD). Warmer sand produces females; cooler sand produces males.

üõ†Ô∏è Conservation: How YOU Can Help

1. Reduce Road Mortality (Freshwater Turtles)

Females of the Red-bellied Turtle are often killed crossing roads to nest. Conservation Action: Barriers and tunnels are sometimes used to guide them safely.

2. Protect Water Quality (Flattened Musk Turtle)

Silt and pollution from runoff destroy the habitat and food source (snails). Conservation Action: Support local watershed protection efforts and erosion control.

3. Keep Beaches Dark (Sea Turtles)

Artificial lighting disorients nesting females and hatchlings. Conservation Action: Use turtle-friendly (red or amber) lighting and turn off all lights visible from the beach during nesting season.
